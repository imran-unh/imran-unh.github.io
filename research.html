Research

Cryosphere • Glaciers • Remote Sensing • Glacial Lakes • Flood Hazards • GLOF Triggers

My research uses satellite remote sensing and geospatial analysis to monitor glacial lakes and supraglacial hydrology in high-mountain environments. A central goal of my work has been to improve glacial lake detection and monitoring using Synthetic Aperture Radar (SAR), to connect observed lake dynamics to glacier-lake-climate interactions, and to understand how climate change impacts outburst flood (GLOF) triggers.

Ph.D. Research Theme

Advancing glacial lake mapping and monitoring

Many high-mountain regions experience persistent cloud cover, seasonal snow, and complex terrain that limit optical-only monitoring. SAR enables more consistent lake mapping through clouds and low-light conditions. My dissertation focuses on developing and evaluating SAR-based methods for glacial lake detection and monitoring, with applications in the Upper Indus Basin.

  • Developing robust lake mapping workflows using Sentinel-1 SAR.
  • Evaluating seasonal and interannual patterns in lake extent and persistence.
  • Connecting lake dynamics to glacier setting, surface conditions, and climatic forcing.

Recent & Ongoing Research Areas

1) SAR-based glacial lake mapping

Building automated and semi-automated approaches for mapping glacial lakes using SAR, aimed at improving consistency across seasons and reducing sensitivity to atmospheric limitations.

  • Sentinel-1 processing and lake classification
  • Regional-scale mapping in mountainous terrain

2) Seasonal supraglacial lake dynamics

Characterizing how supraglacial lakes evolve within melt seasons and across years, including timing, persistence, and drainage behavior, to better understand meltwater routing and storage on glaciers.

  • Multi-year seasonal analysis
  • Links to melt-season conditions and glacier surface state

3) Glacier-lake-climate interactions

Exploring how lake evolution relates to glacier geometry, debris cover, and climate variability, and how these relationships can inform risk assessments for downstream communities.

  • Controls on lake growth and stability
  • Climate and surface-process linkages
  • Implications for long-term hazard trajectories

4) Outburst flood hazards (GLOF)

Using remote sensing evidence and climatic data to diagnose potential triggers and impacts of outburst floods, supporting hazard communication and disaster preparedness.

  • Event diagnosis using multi-source satellite observations
  • Downstream impact mapping and risk communication

Research Interests

  • Operational remote sensing for cryosphere hazard monitoring and early warning
  • Supraglacial hydrology, meltwater routing, and glacier–hydrology coupling
  • Multi-sensor fusion (SAR + optical + DEMs) for robust change detection
  • Assessing the dynamics of glacial lakes and GLOF triggers under climate change
  • Linking physical process understanding to actionable risk assessments
